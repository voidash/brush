Fundamental Chemistry Concepts

Before beginning any chemical work, it's essential to understand the fundamental concepts that will be used throughout this process. These concepts form the foundation of chemical reactions and measurements.

Understanding Molarity (M)

Definition: Molarity is a measure of the concentration of a solute in a solution, expressed as the number of moles of solute per liter of solution.

Formula: M = moles of solute / liters of solution

Example: If you dissolve 5.85 grams of NaCl (molecular weight = 58.5 g/mol) in enough water to make 1 liter of solution, the molarity would be 0.1 M because 5.85g / 58.5 g/mol = 0.1 moles, and 0.1 moles / 1 L = 0.1 M.

Importance: In this process, molarity helps us determine the exact amount of acid or base needed to achieve a specific pH during acidification and basification steps. Using molarity ensures precise chemical ratios for optimal reaction yields.

Understanding Normality (N)

Definition: Normality is another measure of concentration that takes into account the reactive capacity of a chemical. It's expressed as the number of gram equivalents per liter of solution.

Formula: N = Molarity √ó n-factor

n-factor: The number of H‚Å∫ or OH‚Åª ions a molecule can donate/accept in a reaction. For HCl, n-factor = 1. For H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ, n-factor = 2.

Example: A 1 M HCl solution has a normality of 1 N (since it can donate 1 H‚Å∫). A 1 M H‚ÇÇSO‚ÇÑ solution has a normality of 2 N (since it can donate 2 H‚Å∫).

Importance: Normality is particularly useful in acid-base reactions like titration because it directly relates to the reactive capacity, making it easier to calculate required volumes for complete neutralization.

Titration Explained

Definition: Titration is a laboratory technique used to determine the unknown concentration of a solution by reacting it with a solution of known concentration (the titrant) until the reaction is complete.

Key Components:

  • Analyte: The solution with unknown concentration (what we're trying to measure)
  • Titrant: The solution with known concentration (standard solution)
  • Indicator: A substance that changes color when the reaction is complete (endpoint)
  • Burette: Graduated glass tube used to deliver precise volumes of titrant

The Process:

  1. Measure a known volume of the analyte and place it in a flask
  2. Add a few drops of indicator
  3. Slowly add titrant from the burette while swirling the flask
  4. Stop when the indicator changes color (endpoint reached)
  5. Calculate the unknown concentration using the titration data

Calculation Formula: N‚ÇÅV‚ÇÅ = N‚ÇÇV‚ÇÇ (where N = normality, V = volume, subscript 1 = analyte, subscript 2 = titrant)

Example: To determine the concentration of an unknown HCl solution, you might titrate it against a standard NaOH solution. If 25 mL of 0.1 N NaOH is required to neutralize 20 mL of the unknown HCl, then N(HCl) √ó 20 mL = 0.1 N √ó 25 mL, so N(HCl) = 0.125 N.

Importance in this Process: You'll need to understand titration to verify acid/base concentrations, determine when pH adjustments are complete, and ensure proper stoichiometric ratios in reactions.

Laws of Stoichiometry

Definition: Stoichiometry is the calculation of reactants and products in chemical reactions based on the balanced chemical equation.

Law of Conservation of Mass: In any chemical reaction, mass is neither created nor destroyed. The total mass of reactants equals the total mass of products.

Law of Definite Proportions: A given chemical compound always contains exactly the same proportion of elements by mass.

Law of Multiple Proportions: When two elements form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other are in ratios of small whole numbers.

Practical Application:

When balancing the reduction reaction in Step 4, we need to ensure the stoichiometric ratios are correct. The reaction can be represented as:

Pseudoephedrine (C‚ÇÅ‚ÇÄH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÖNO) + Red P + I‚ÇÇ ‚Üí Methamphetamine + PI‚ÇÉ byproducts

To calculate the correct amounts:

  • Write the balanced chemical equation
  • Determine the molar mass of each reactant (pseudoephedrine = 165.23 g/mol)
  • Calculate moles needed based on desired yield
  • Convert moles to grams for practical measurement

Example Calculation: If you want to produce 10 grams of methamphetamine (molar mass = 149.23 g/mol):

  • Moles of meth desired = 10g / 149.23 g/mol = 0.067 mol
  • Moles of pseudoephedrine needed = 0.067 mol (1:1 ratio in ideal case)
  • Mass of pseudoephedrine = 0.067 mol √ó 165.23 g/mol = 11.07 grams

Limiting Reactant: The reactant that is completely consumed first determines the maximum amount of product that can be formed. Always identify your limiting reactant to predict yield accurately.

pH Scale and Acid-Base Chemistry

Definition: pH is a measure of how acidic or basic a solution is, on a scale from 0-14.

  • pH < 7: Acidic (more H‚Å∫ ions)
  • pH = 7: Neutral
  • pH > 7: Basic/Alkaline (more OH‚Åª ions)

Important pH Points in this Process:

  • pH 1-2: Highly acidic - used in acidification step (Step 5) to convert free base to salt
  • pH 7: Neutral - typically used for washing steps
  • pH 11-12: Strongly basic - used in basification step (Step 6) to convert salt back to free base

How to Adjust pH:

  • To lower pH (make acidic): Add acid (HCl) dropwise while stirring and monitoring with pH meter or paper
  • To raise pH (make basic): Add base (NaOH) dropwise while stirring and monitoring with pH meter or paper

Importance: The solubility of methamphetamine changes dramatically with pH. It's water-soluble in acidic conditions (salt form) and soluble in organic solvents in basic conditions (free base). This property is the basis for the purification steps.

Solution Preparation Techniques

Preparing Solutions of Known Concentration:

  1. Calculate the mass needed: mass = molarity √ó volume (L) √ó molar mass
  2. Weigh the calculated mass using a precision balance
  3. Transfer to a volumetric flask or graduated cylinder
  4. Add solvent (usually water) about halfway to final volume
  5. Swirl to dissolve completely
  6. Add solvent to the calibration mark
  7. Mix thoroughly by inverting several times

Dilution: To dilute a concentrated solution to a lower concentration:

C‚ÇÅV‚ÇÅ = C‚ÇÇV‚ÇÇ (where C = concentration, V = volume)

Example: To prepare 500 mL of 1 M HCl from 6 M HCl:

6 M √ó V‚ÇÅ = 1 M √ó 500 mL, so V‚ÇÅ = 83.3 mL of concentrated HCl

Measure 83.3 mL of 6 M HCl, then add water to reach 500 mL total volume.

Reading a Balance and Making Precise Measurements

Using an Analytical Balance:

  • Ensure the balance is on a stable, vibration-free surface
  • Allow the balance to warm up (usually 30 minutes to 1 hour)
  • Press the tare button to zero the balance
  • Place weighing paper or container on the balance
  • Tare again to zero with the container
  • Add the substance slowly until you reach the desired mass
  • Wait for the reading to stabilize before recording

Accuracy Tips:

  • Always use clean, dry containers
  • Close drafts (close doors, windows, turn off fans)
  • Never place hot or cold objects directly on the balance
  • Use the smallest practical container to minimize error
  • For powders, use weighing boats or paper
  • For liquids, use pipettes or graduated cylinders

Understanding Filtration

Filtration: The process of separating solids from liquids by passing the mixture through a filter medium that allows the liquid to pass but retains the solid particles.

Types of Filtration:

  • Gravity Filtration: Uses gravity to pull liquid through filter paper. Good for large volumes and when time is not critical.
  • Vacuum Filtration: Uses vacuum pressure to speed up filtration. Faster and can remove more liquid from the solid.
  • Hot Filtration: Performed when the solution is hot to prevent premature crystallization.

Setup for Vacuum Filtration:

  • Connect vacuum pump to side arm of Buchner flask
  • Place filter paper in Buchner funnel
  • Wet the filter paper with solvent to seal it
  • Pour the mixture into the funnel
  • Turn on vacuum
  • Rinse solid with cold solvent to remove impurities
  • Continue vacuum until solid is dry

Now that you understand the fundamental chemical concepts, ensure you have all necessary equipment and a properly ventilated workspace.

Required Equipment and Their Uses

  • Laboratory-grade glassware (round-bottom flasks, condensers): Heat-resistant glass containers for holding and heating reaction mixtures. Round-bottom flasks distribute heat evenly and prevent hotspots that could damage the reaction.
  • Heating mantle or hot plate with temperature control: Provides uniform heating. A heating mantle surrounds the flask for better heat distribution than a hot plate. Temperature control (¬±1¬∞C accuracy) is critical for maintaining optimal reaction conditions.
  • Vacuum pump: Creates low pressure conditions for vacuum filtration and distillation. Essential for removing solvents efficiently and for drying solid products.
  • Separatory funnel (250mL-500mL): A conical-shaped glass container with a stopcock at the bottom, used for separating immiscible liquids (liquids that don't mix, like oil and water). The bottom layer is drained first through the stopcock.
  • Filter paper and Buchner funnel: Used together for vacuum filtration. The Buchner funnel connects to a flask that attaches to a vacuum pump. Filter paper (medium porosity, 11cm diameter) fits inside to trap solids.
  • Precision balance (¬±0.1g accuracy): An electronic scale capable of measuring very small masses with high precision. Critical for accurate stoichiometric calculations. Calibrate regularly using standard weights.
  • Thermometer: Measures reaction temperature. Use a digital thermometer or glass thermometer calibrated in Celsius. Place it so it's immersed in the reaction mixture but not touching the glass bottom.
  • pH meter: Electronic device for measuring pH precisely. Calibrate before use using pH 4, 7, and 10 buffer solutions. Rinse electrode with distilled water between measurements.
  • Safety equipment (gloves, goggles, fume hood): Nitrile gloves protect skin from chemical contact. Chemical splash goggles prevent eye injury. Fume hood exhausts toxic fumes and provides containment in case of spills or vapors.
  • Magnetic stirrer and stir bars: Provides continuous mixing without manual intervention. Drop a small PTFE-coated magnetic stir bar into the flask; the stirrer's rotating magnet makes it spin, mixing the contents.
  • Pipettes and graduated cylinders: For precise volume measurements. Use appropriate-sized cylinders (usually measuring to the middle of the range for best accuracy). Pipettes are for small volumes (usually <10 mL).
  • Rotary evaporator (optional but recommended): Efficiently removes solvents under reduced pressure, preserving heat-sensitive compounds. Uses a water bath to heat and vacuum to lower boiling point.

Chemical Reagents and Their Properties

  • Pseudoephedrine or ephedrine tablets: The starting material. Pseudoephedrine HCl (molecular weight: 201.70 g/mol for the salt, 165.23 g/mol for free base) contains binders and fillers that must be removed.
  • Red phosphorus: Amorphous red phosphorus (molecular weight: 30.97 g/mol) serves as the reducing agent. Powder form increases surface area for reaction. Handle carefully as it can form phosphine gas when reduced.
  • Iodine crystals: Purple crystalline solid (molecular weight: 253.81 g/mol, melting point: 114¬∞C). Sublimes (goes directly from solid to vapor) at room temperature. Store in tightly sealed, amber-colored container. Iodine reacts with phosphorus to form phosphorus triiodide (PI‚ÇÉ), the actual reducing agent.
  • Hydrochloric acid (HCl): Concentrated (~37% w/w, 12 M) or diluted solutions (commonly 5-6 M for use). Strong acid (pKa ‚âà -7). Used for acidification and converting free base to salt form. Corrosive - handle with care.
  • Sodium hydroxide (NaOH): Pellets or flakes (molecular weight: 40.00 g/mol). Strong base. Dissolves exothermically in water (generates heat). Used for basification steps. Store in airtight container as it absorbs moisture from air.
  • Toluene: Organic solvent (molecular weight: 92.14 g/mol, boiling point: 111¬∞C). Non-polar, good for extracting non-polar compounds. Flammable - keep away from ignition sources.
  • Acetone: Organic solvent (molecular weight: 58.08 g/mol, boiling point: 56¬∞C). Used for recrystallization and washing. Highly volatile and flammable.
  • Isopropanol (isopropyl alcohol): Solvent (molecular weight: 60.10 g/mol, boiling point: 82¬∞C). Used for extraction and washing. 70% or 99% purity available.
  • Distilled water: Pure water with impurities removed. Essential for all aqueous solutions and washes to avoid introducing contaminants.
  • Anhydrous magnesium sulfate: Drying agent (molecular weight: 120.37 g/mol). Absorbs water from organic solutions. "Anhydrous" means "without water." After use, it can be regenerated by heating to remove absorbed water.

Extraction of Pseudoephedrine from Tablets

The first chemical operation is to extract pure pseudoephedrine from commercial tablets, which contain binders, fillers, and other inactive ingredients that would interfere with the reduction reaction. This step uses principles of solubility and crystallization.

Understanding Solubility

Principle: "Like dissolves like" - polar solvents dissolve polar substances, non-polar solvents dissolve non-polar substances.

  • Polar solvents: Water, isopropanol, acetone - good for ionic or polar compounds
  • Non-polar solvents: Toluene, hexane - good for non-polar organic compounds

Pseudoephedrine is moderately soluble in hot isopropanol but insoluble in cold isopropanol. Many binders and fillers remain insoluble even in hot solvent.

Solubility Temperature Dependence: Most solids become more soluble as temperature increases. We'll exploit this by dissolving pseudoephedrine in hot solvent, then cooling to force it to crystallize out while impurities stay dissolved.

Detailed Extraction Procedure

Step 3.1: Tablet Preparation

  • Calculate the number of tablets needed. For example, if using 60mg pseudoephedrine tablets and wanting 10g of pseudoephedrine: 10,000mg √∑ 60mg = 167 tablets. (In practice, account for yield losses - you might need ~200 tablets).
  • Inspect tablets - they should not be broken, discolored, or expired. Remove any outer coating if present (can be scraped off with a razor blade).

Step 3.2: Crushing Tablets

  • Work in small batches (10-20 tablets at a time) for more thorough grinding.
  • Place tablets in a mortar and add a small amount of clean sand or table salt (acts as grinding aid to improve efficiency).
  • Grind using the pestle in a circular motion, applying moderate pressure. Continue until tablets are reduced to a fine, uniform powder (no visible chunks).
  • Transfer powder to a clean container. Repeat until all tablets are crushed.
  • Combine all powdered material and mix thoroughly to ensure uniformity.

Step 3.3: Hot Extraction

  • Measure isopropanol (99% purity preferred). Use approximately 5-6 mL per gram of expected pseudoephedrine yield. For 10g, use 50-60 mL of isopropanol.
  • Pour the isopropanol into a large beaker (500mL or 1000mL) and place on a hot plate.
  • Heat to 70-80¬∞C (just below boiling). Do NOT boil - isopropanol boils at 82¬∞C and boiling creates too much vapor. A temperature of ~75¬∞C is ideal.
  • Gradually add the powdered tablet mixture to the hot isopropanol while stirring continuously with a glass rod. Add slowly to prevent clumping.
  • Once all powder is added, maintain temperature at 70-80¬∞C and stir for 15-20 minutes. This allows pseudoephedrine to dissolve completely.
  • What's happening: Pseudoephedrine dissolves in hot isopropanol, while binders (cellulose, starches) and fillers (calcium carbonate, magnesium stearate) remain as insoluble solids.

Step 3.4: Filtration (Removal of Insoluble Fillers)

  • Prepare a filtration setup: Place a funnel lined with filter paper in a clean flask. For vacuum filtration, use Buchner funnel and flask connected to vacuum pump.
  • Pre-wet the filter paper with a small amount of hot isopropanol (helps it adhere and improves flow).
  • Pour the hot mixture through the filter. If the first batch slows down too much, wait for some to filter before adding more.
  • After all liquid has passed through, rinse the solid residue in the filter with small amounts (~10 mL each) of hot isopropanol (2-3 times) to recover any remaining pseudoephedrine.
  • The filtrate (the liquid that passed through) contains dissolved pseudoephedrine. The solid residue in the filter (retentate) contains fillers and binders - dispose of as solid waste.
  • Observation: The filtrate should be clear or slightly yellowish. If cloudy, re-filter through fresh filter paper.

Step 3.5: Cooling and Crystallization

  • Transfer the filtrate to a clean beaker and place it in an ice bath (beaker filled with ice and water).
  • Stir occasionally with a glass rod.
  • Crystals will begin to form as the solution cools below 40¬∞C. Continue cooling in ice bath for 15-20 minutes.
  • What's happening: As temperature decreases, pseudoephedrine becomes less soluble in isopropanol and precipitates out as crystals. This is the principle of recrystallization.
  • For maximum yield, after the initial ice bath, place the beaker in a refrigerator (4-8¬∞C) for an additional 30-60 minutes, then in a freezer (-18¬∞C) for 1-2 hours. Lower temperatures force more pseudoephedrine out of solution.

Step 3.6: Collecting Crystals

  • Set up vacuum filtration with Buchner funnel, filter paper, and vacuum flask.
  • Pour the cold mixture (both liquid and crystals) into the funnel. The vacuum will pull liquid through quickly, leaving crystals on the filter paper.
  • Wash the crystals with cold isopropanol (10-15 mL) to remove any impurities clinging to the crystal surfaces. The wash should be ice-cold to prevent dissolving the crystals.
  • Continue vacuum for 2-3 minutes after liquid stops dripping to remove as much solvent as possible from crystals.

Step 3.7: Drying

  • Transfer crystals to a watch glass or shallow dish.
  • Place in a desiccator (a sealed container with drying agent like silica gel or calcium chloride) for 24 hours to remove residual solvent.
  • Alternatively, place in a low-temperature oven (50-60¬∞C) for several hours, checking periodically to ensure crystals don't melt or discolor.
  • Test for dryness: Weigh a sample, then place it in oven for 15 more minutes and reweigh. If weight hasn't changed, it's dry.

Step 3.8: Quality Assessment and Yield Calculation

  • Weigh the dried pseudoephedrine crystals.
  • Expected yield: Approximately 35-40mg per 60mg tablet (60-67% recovery). From 200 tablets, expect ~7-8 grams.
  • Appearance: White to off-white crystalline solid. Yellowish color indicates impurities - may need recrystallization.
  • Test for purity: Melting point of pure pseudoephedrine HCl is 118-119¬∞C. If melting range is broad or lower, impurities are present.

Common Problems and Solutions

Problem: Very low yield (<50% expected)

Causes:

  • Insufficient heating - pseudoephedrine not fully dissolved
  • Filtration too slow - pseudoephedrine crystallized in filter
  • Inadequate cooling - not enough pseudoephedrine precipitated
  • Crystals lost during transfer

Solutions: Ensure proper temperature control, use vacuum filtration for speed, cool thoroughly, and rinse vessels with cold solvent to recover crystals.

Problem: Crystals are yellow/brown colored

Cause: Impurities co-crystallized with pseudoephedrine

Solution: Recrystallize - dissolve crude product in minimal hot isopropanol, filter, and cool again. Repeat if necessary.

Problem: Filtrate is very cloudy after hot filtration

Cause: Fine particles passed through filter

Solution: Re-filter through fresh filter paper or use finer porosity filter paper.

Optional: Recrystallization for Higher Purity

If pseudoephedrine appears impure (colored, broad melting range), perform recrystallization:

  • Dissolve crude pseudoephedrine in minimal hot isopropanol (just enough to dissolve when hot)
  • Filter hot solution to remove insoluble impurities
  • Cool slowly to room temperature, then place in ice bath
  • Collect crystals by vacuum filtration
  • Wash with cold isopropanol and dry
  • Yield will decrease (~70-80% of crude), but purity increases significantly
Safety Notes: Isopropanol vapors are flammable. Keep away from ignition sources. Work in fume hood. Hot isopropanol can cause severe burns - handle with care. Use heat-resistant gloves when handling hot glassware. The tablet powder may contain fine particulates that are harmful if inhaled - avoid creating dust clouds, consider wearing a dust mask during crushing.

Workup - Understanding Liquid-Liquid Extraction

After the reduction reaction is complete, we must separate the desired product (methamphetamine) from the reaction mixture. This involves liquid-liquid extraction, a fundamental technique based on the principle that different compounds have different solubilities in different solvents.

Principles of Liquid-Liquid Extraction

Definition: Liquid-liquid extraction separates compounds based on their relative solubility in two immiscible (non-mixing) liquids.

Immiscible Liquids: Two liquids that do not mix to form a homogeneous solution. When combined, they form two distinct layers based on density.

Density Determines Layer Order:

  • Denser liquid: Sinks to bottom (bottom layer)
  • Less dense liquid: Floats on top (top layer)

Common Densities:

  • Water: 1.0 g/mL
  • Toluene: 0.87 g/mL (less dense than water - top layer)
  • Dichloromethane: 1.33 g/mL (denser than water - bottom layer)
  • Diethyl ether: 0.71 g/mL (less dense than water - top layer)

Distribution Coefficient (Kd or Partition Coefficient):

Kd = [compound] in organic phase / [compound] in aqueous phase

Interpretation:

  • Kd >> 1: Compound prefers organic phase
  • Kd << 1: Compound prefers aqueous phase
  • Kd ‚âà 1: Compounds distributed equally between phases

The Role of pH in Extraction

Key Concept: The solubility of many organic compounds changes dramatically with pH, particularly compounds containing amine groups like methamphetamine.

Amines and Acid-Base Behavior:

  • Free base form (neutral): Organic compound without charge. Generally soluble in organic solvents (toluene, ether), insoluble in water.
  • Salt form (charged): Protonated amine (ammonium ion). Generally water-soluble, insoluble in organic solvents.

For Methamphetamine:

  • At high pH (> 11): Exists as free base (C‚ÇÅ‚ÇÄH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÖN). Soluble in toluene, insoluble in water.
  • At low pH (< 2): Exists as ammonium salt (C‚ÇÅ‚ÇÄH‚ÇÅ‚ÇÖNH‚Å∫ Cl‚Åª). Soluble in water, insoluble in toluene.
  • At intermediate pH: Some of both forms exist, leading to partial solubility in both phases.

pH Manipulation for Extraction:

  • Acidification: Add acid ‚Üí lower pH ‚Üí convert to salt ‚Üí extract into aqueous phase
  • Basification: Add base ‚Üí raise pH ‚Üí convert to free base ‚Üí extract into organic phase

This is the principle we'll use repeatedly: Alternately convert between free base and salt forms to separate product from impurities and purify it.

Understanding the Separatory Funnel

Anatomy of a Separatory Funnel:

  • Stopcock: Valve at bottom that opens/closes to allow controlled draining of bottom layer
  • Stopper: Cap at top (should be loosened before opening stopcock to prevent pressure buildup)
  • Stem: Tube below stopcock where liquid drains
  • Conical body: Main container where separation occurs

Using the Separatory Funnel:

  1. Check the stopcock: Ensure it's closed before adding liquids
  2. Add the mixture: Pour in the liquid to be separated
  3. Add extraction solvent: Add the immiscible solvent that will extract your target compound
  4. Stopper the funnel: Place stopper securely (but not overly tight)
  5. Vent the funnel: Invert the funnel, open stopcock to release pressure (essential if using volatile solvents or acid/base reactions)
  6. Shake gently: While inverted, gently shake the funnel several times
  7. Vent again: After shaking, release pressure before turning right-side up
  8. Allow to separate: Set funnel in ring stand, allow layers to separate completely (may take 1-5 minutes)
  9. Drain bottom layer: Place receiving flask under stem, open stopcock slowly, drain bottom layer
  10. Collect top layer: When bottom layer is completely drained, close stopcock, pour top layer out through top
  11. Repeat if needed: Multiple extractions with fresh solvent improve yield

Critical Tip - Venting:

ALWAYS vent the separatory funnel after shaking. Gases produced (CO‚ÇÇ from acid + carbonate, or vapor pressure) can create enough pressure to force stopper out, spraying contents. Venting prevents this dangerous situation.

Step 4.1: Cooling and Quenching the Reaction

  • After the reduction reaction is complete (see Step 5), turn off heating mantle.
  • Allow reaction mixture to cool to room temperature. This typically takes 30-60 minutes.
  • Do not add cold water to hot reaction mixture - this can cause violent reactions and splashing.
  • Once at room temperature, slowly add 200-300 mL of distilled water while stirring.
  • This "quenches" the reaction by diluting reactants and stopping further chemical changes.
  • Observation: The mixture will likely separate into two phases - an organic solvent layer (toluene or methanol) and an aqueous water layer.

Step 4.2: Transferring to Separatory Funnel

  • Place a large separatory funnel (500mL-1000mL) in a ring stand.
  • Ensure stopcock is closed.
  • Pour the cooled reaction mixture into the separatory funnel.
  • Important: Do not fill the funnel more than 2/3 full. Leave headspace for shaking and expansion.
  • If necessary, transfer in batches to avoid overfilling.
  • Rinse the reaction flask with a small amount of water or solvent and add the rinse to the funnel to recover any residual product.

Step 4.3: Identifying Layers

  • Set the funnel aside and allow the layers to separate completely. This may take 1-5 minutes.
  • If using toluene: Toluene (density 0.87) will be the TOP layer. Aqueous layer (density 1.0) will be BOTTOM.
  • If using dichloromethane: DCM (density 1.33) will be the BOTTOM layer. Aqueous layer will be TOP.
  • Visual identification:

    • Organic layer: Often colored (yellowish, brownish) from iodine residues
    • Aqueous layer: Usually clear or slightly cloudy, may have different color
  • Test if uncertain: Add a drop of each layer to a small amount of water in a test tube. The layer that mixes is aqueous; the one that separates is organic.

Step 4.4: Initial Liquid-Liquid Extraction

  • Goal: Separate the organic solvent (which contains product) from aqueous residues.
  • Stopper the separatory funnel.
  • Invert the funnel (hold both stopper and stopcock).
  • Open the stopcock briefly to vent any pressure (listen for the hiss of escaping gas).
  • Close the stopcock and gently shake the funnel for 10-15 seconds while inverted.
  • Vent again (important - gases may have built up from shaking).
  • Set funnel upright in stand and allow layers to separate (1-3 minutes).
  • Position a receiving flask (appropriately labeled) under the stem.
  • Open the stopcock slowly and drain the BOTTOM layer.
  • Close stopcock just before the interface between layers reaches the stopcock.
  • Collect the top layer by pouring it out through the top of the funnel into a separate container.
  • Which to keep: Keep the organic layer (the solvent you added - toluene or methanol). The aqueous layer contains inorganic byproducts (phosphorous acid, phosphates, etc.) and can be discarded.

Step 4.5: Washing the Organic Layer

  • Purpose: Remove residual inorganic impurities from the organic phase.
  • Return the organic layer to the separatory funnel.
  • Add an equal volume of distilled water.
  • Stopper, invert, vent, shake gently for 10 seconds, vent again.
  • Allow to separate.
  • Drain the aqueous layer (bottom layer - always water).
  • Repeat washing 2-3 times with fresh water each time.
  • After final wash, the organic layer should be cleaner (lighter color).

Step 4.6: Acidification - Converting Free Base to Salt

  • Goal: Convert methamphetamine free base (in organic layer) to water-soluble methamphetamine hydrochloride salt.
  • Transfer the washed organic layer to a clean beaker or flask.
  • Prepare 100-150 mL of 6 M HCl (hydrochloric acid).
  • IMPORTANT: Always add acid to water, never water to acid. If you don't have pre-made 6 M HCl, dilute concentrated (12 M) HCl by adding equal parts water (slowly) while stirring and cooling.
  • Begin adding the HCl to the organic layer slowly while stirring continuously.
  • Add dropwise at first: The acidification is exothermic (produces heat). Add slowly initially to control heat generation.
  • Observe: As you add acid, you should see the mixture become more aqueous. The organic layer may shrink as product transfers to water.
  • Monitoring pH: Use pH meter or pH paper. Continue adding acid until pH reaches 1-2 (strongly acidic).
  • Typical volume: For 10g scale, 100-150 mL of 6 M HCl is usually sufficient, but monitor pH to be sure.

Step 4.7: Separating the Aqueous Acidic Layer

  • After acidification, transfer the mixture back to the separatory funnel.
  • Allow layers to separate completely.
  • Now, the AQUEOUS layer (pH 1-2) contains the product as methamphetamine hydrochloride.
  • The organic layer (solvent) may still contain some iodine residues or other byproducts.
  • Drain the bottom layer (which will be aqueous if using toluene) into a clean flask.
  • CORRECT THIS: If using toluene, aqueous layer is BOTTOM - drain this. If using DCM, aqueous layer is TOP - pour this out from top.
  • Keep the aqueous acidic layer (contains product). Discard the organic layer.

Step 4.8: Confirming Transfer

  • The aqueous acidic layer should be clear to slightly yellow.
  • To confirm product transfer, you can test a small sample: evaporate a few mL on a watch glass. If white crystals remain, product is present.
  • Yield at this stage: The aqueous solution contains the product as dissolved methamphetamine hydrochloride.

Understanding What Just Happened

Chemical Transformation:

Methamphetamine (free base) + HCl ‚Üí Methamphetamine hydrochloride (salt)

Solubility Change:

  • Free base: Soluble in toluene/DCM, insoluble in water
  • Salt: Soluble in water, insoluble in toluene/DCM

Practical Result: Product moved from organic solvent into aqueous solution, separating it from non-polar impurities that remained in the organic phase.

This is the "Acid Workup": Converting to salt form allows aqueous extraction, removing many organic-soluble impurities. Next, we'll convert back to free base for further purification.

Common Problems and Solutions

Problem: Emulsion Formation

Description: Layers don't separate cleanly - instead, you get a cloudy, milky mixture between distinct layers.

Causes: Presence of surfactants, fine solid particles, or vigorous shaking creating stable emulsion.

Solutions:

  • Wait longer - emulsions often resolve given time (10-30 minutes)
  • Add saturated NaCl solution (brine) - salt helps break emulsions
  • Filter the mixture through filter paper to remove solids
  • Add a small amount of ethanol to help break the emulsion
  • Centrifuge if available

Problem: Unclear which layer is which

Solutions:

  • Add a drop of each layer to separate test tubes containing water. The one that mixes is aqueous.
  • Check known densities: toluene (0.87) < water (1.0) < DCM (1.33)
  • Color can help: organic layer often colored from iodine residues

Problem: Low product transfer during acidification

Causes: pH not low enough, insufficient HCl, product already partially protonated

Solutions:

  • Check pH - ensure it's 1-2
  • Add more HCl and monitor pH
  • Make sure you're using concentrated enough HCl (6 M recommended)

Problem: Significant heat generation during acidification

Cause: Neutralization of base generates heat

Solution: Add HCl more slowly, use ice bath to cool if needed

Safety Reminders:
  • ALWAYS vent the separatory funnel after shaking to prevent pressure buildup
  • Acidification is exothermic - add acid slowly with stirring
  • Wear goggles and gloves when handling acids
  • Work in fume hood - iodine vapors may be present

  • Don't overfill separatory funnel - leave headspace
  • Use appropriate receiving vessels - label all clearly

Basification and Extraction - Converting Salt Back to Free Base

Now that we have methamphetamine as its water-soluble hydrochloride salt, we'll convert it back to the free base form and extract it into an organic solvent.

Chemical Transformation

Reaction: Methamphetamine¬∑HCl (salt) + NaOH ‚Üí Methamphetamine (free base) + NaCl + H‚ÇÇO

Process: Add base to raise pH from acidic to basic (pH 11-12)

Result: Free base precipitates or forms oil layer, then extracted into organic solvent

Detailed Procedure

  • Cool acidic aqueous solution in ice bath
  • Slowly add 10% NaOH solution with stirring, monitoring pH
  • Continue until pH reaches 11-12
  • Free base product will precipitate or form oily layer
  • Add non-polar solvent (toluene or DCM)
  • Shake or stir vigorously to extract product into organic layer
  • Separate organic layer (contains free base)
  • Dry organic layer with anhydrous magnesium sulfate
  • Filter to remove drying agent
Safety: Base addition generates significant heat. Add slowly and maintain cooling. The free base is volatile - keep containers sealed.

Recrystallization for Further Purification

Crystals from Step 6 can be further purified through recrystallization.

Recrystallization Principles

Process: Dissolve in minimal hot solvent, cool slowly to form pure crystals

Solvent: Isopropanol (95-99%) works well

Why it works: Impurities stay dissolved or form separate crystals

Procedure

  • Assess purity - check color (should be white to off-white)
  • Dissolve crystals in minimal hot isopropanol (5-10 mL per gram)
  • Filter hot solution to remove insoluble impurities
  • Cool slowly to room temperature (30-60 minutes)
  • Further cool in ice bath (15-20 minutes)
  • For maximum yield, refrigerate (30 min), then freeze (1-2 hours)
  • Collect crystals by vacuum filtration
  • Wash with cold isopropanol
  • Dry in desiccator or low-temp oven
  • Yield: ~70-80% of crude material
